Lectures are awkward to cite because they are not all the same kind of source. A live class lecture you heard once, a slide deck posted in Canvas, a recorded webinar on YouTube, and a keynote speech published on a conference site all need slightly different treatment. The core question is not "lecture or not?" It is "can my reader retrieve the lecture?"

> The shortest answer: cite a public or course-posted lecture in the reference list. Cite a private lecture you heard in class as personal communication or as an in-person presentation, depending on the style your teacher assigned.

## Decide what kind of lecture you have

Start by sorting the source into one of these shapes:

- **Live class lecture heard in person** - not publicly retrievable unless your instructor posted notes or a recording.
- **Lecture slides or notes posted in an LMS** - retrievable for classmates or instructors with access to Canvas, Blackboard, Moodle, Google Classroom, or another course site.
- **Recorded lecture from a course site** - retrievable for a limited audience; often cited like online course material.
- **Public online lecture or speech** - retrievable to anyone with the URL, such as a TED talk, webinar, conference video, or university lecture page.
- **Lecture you watched on YouTube** - cite as an online video, not as private class material.
- **Conference paper or presentation** - cite as a conference presentation if the lecture was part of a meeting, symposium, or poster session.

APA's lecture-note guidance makes the access distinction explicit: slides available online can be referenced with the site and URL, but slides from a classroom website should be cited that way only when the audience can access them. If the audience cannot access them, APA says to cite them as personal communication.

MLA frames course materials through the MLA core-elements template. Its course-materials guidance says to use the known author when you know who created the material, to use an LMS as a container when it is the place where the course work was collected, and to cite an in-person lecture like an in-person presentation.

Chicago treats lectures, meeting papers, and similar presentations together: include the presenter, title when there is one, medium or presentation type, sponsorship or course/event, location, and date. If the lecture is published online, add the URL.

## Information to collect before you cite

Write down the details before you open the generator or build the citation by hand:

- **Presenter or instructor** - the person who delivered the lecture.
- **Title or topic** - use the formal lecture title if supplied; otherwise use a short description.
- **Course, event, or series** - the class name, conference, webinar series, or university lecture series.
- **Format** - lecture, PowerPoint slides, lecture notes, video recording, webinar, keynote, or conference presentation.
- **Date** - the lecture date, posting date, or date the course material was made available.
- **Container** - Canvas, Blackboard, Moodle, YouTube, TED, the conference site, or the university website.
- **Institution or location** - school, organization, city, or campus when relevant.
- **URL** - use the stable URL or login URL when your reader has access.
- **Timestamp or slide number** - needed when quoting a specific moment or slide.

Do not assume your instructor wrote a slide deck just because they uploaded it. MLA specifically warns against listing the instructor as author unless you know the instructor created the material. If a course site merely links to an outside article, video, or ebook, cite the original source type instead.

In-text examples:

- APA: (Chen, 2026)
- MLA: (Chen)
- Chicago author-date: (Chen 2026)
- Harvard: (Chen, 2026)
- Vancouver: (1)
- IEEE: [1]
- AMA: superscript reference number

When you quote a specific moment from a recorded lecture, add a timestamp in the in-text citation or sentence: "Chen argues that the risk is 'not only technical' (12:14)." For slides, use slide numbers if the deck has them.

## How to cite a live class lecture

A live lecture that was never posted online is not retrievable by readers. That changes the citation.

**APA.** Cite it as personal communication in the text only, unless the lecture notes or slides are available to your audience. Example:

> (M. Chen, personal communication, March 4, 2026)

**MLA.** MLA can include an in-person lecture in the works cited. Use the instructor, course name or lecture title, date, institution, and the label "Lecture."

> Chen, Maya. "AI in Public Health Surveillance." Data Ethics 240, 4 Mar. 2026, Harbor University. Lecture.

**Chicago.** Chicago can cite the lecture in a note and bibliography. If there is no formal title, skip the title or use a clear description.

> Chen, Maya. "AI in Public Health Surveillance." Lecture, Data Ethics 240, Harbor University, March 4, 2026.

**Harvard.** Many Harvard variants include lectures and course materials in the reference list, labeled by format. Your school may have a local rule, so follow the handbook your instructor named.

**Vancouver, IEEE, and AMA.** Treat non-retrievable lectures like personal communication or private communication. Mention them in the text and avoid adding a normal reference-list entry unless your instructor requires one.

## How to cite lecture slides

Lecture slides are usually easier than live lectures because the slide deck has a title, author, and location.

APA uses a bracketed description such as `[PowerPoint slides]` after the title. If the slides are on a public site, include the site name and URL. If they are on a classroom website or intranet and your audience has access, include the LMS name and login URL. If your audience cannot access the slides, cite them as personal communication.

MLA treats the LMS as a container when the slides were posted there for the course. Use the creator if known, title of the slide deck, course or LMS container, posting date when available, URL, and the format.

Chicago treats slides as a presentation or course material. Include the speaker, title, format, course or event, institution, date, and URL when available.

If the slides quote a study, book, article, image, or dataset, do not cite the slide deck unless your point is about the lecture itself. Find the original source and cite that. APA's guidance says the same thing for slides that contain citations to other sources.

## Public lecture, course lecture, or YouTube video?

Use the source type that best describes the version you used:

- A lecture posted on a university page is a public online lecture.
- A recording inside Canvas or Blackboard is course material.
- A video on YouTube is an online video. Use [How to Cite a YouTube Video](/guides/how-to-cite-a-youtube-video/).
- A conference talk that later appeared in published proceedings may be cited as a chapter or paper in proceedings.
- A lecture transcript published as an article should be cited as the article or transcript.

This distinction matters for SEO-style citation examples too: the author, container, and date can change depending on the version. If the same lecture appears both in Canvas and on YouTube, cite the version you actually used.

## Common mistakes

**Inventing a lecture title.** If the lecture has no title, use a plain description instead.

**Citing Canvas when Canvas only linked to another source.** If the course page only linked to a journal article, ebook, or public video, cite the original source.

**Putting private lectures in every reference list.** APA, Vancouver, IEEE, and AMA generally keep private, non-retrievable communications out of the reference list.

**Forgetting the format.** Add labels such as lecture, video lecture, PowerPoint slides, or course handout when the format helps a reader identify the source.

**Using the wrong date.** For course materials, use the lecture date, posting date, or date made available to the class, depending on the style and the version you used.

**Skipping timestamps.** For recorded lectures, timestamps are the equivalent of page numbers when you quote a precise moment.

## Using the generator for lectures

The [citation generator](/) works best when the lecture has a public URL, clean title, and visible publication date. For LMS pages, private slides, and live lectures, you will usually need to add details manually after generating a draft.

Check these fields before submitting:

1. The presenter is in the author position.
2. The lecture title or topic is accurate.
3. The source is labeled as lecture, video lecture, or slides.
4. The course, institution, platform, or event is included when relevant.
5. The date is the date of the lecture or the version you used.
6. The URL is accessible to your intended reader, or the citation is treated as personal communication.

The main rule is simple: if readers can find the lecture, give them a path to it. If they cannot, cite it as the communication it was.
