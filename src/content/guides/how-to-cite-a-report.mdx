Reports sit between books and websites. They are usually standalone works, often published by organizations, frequently distributed as PDFs, and full of details that matter in citations: report numbers, issuing agencies, parent agencies, series names, and access dates. If you cite them as generic web pages, the entry loses the information that makes the report traceable.

> The shortest answer: cite the report as a report, not as a PDF. Collect the organization or author, title, year, report number, publisher or issuing body, URL, and access date if your style requires one.

## What counts as a report

A report is a standalone document issued by an organization, agency, company, research group, or institution. Common examples include:

- Government reports
- Annual reports
- Technical reports
- White papers
- Policy briefs
- Working papers
- Research institute reports
- Nonprofit reports
- Corporate sustainability or financial reports

The file format does not decide the citation type. A PDF can be a report, a journal article, a dissertation, a book chapter, a form, or a scanned letter. Look at the source itself. If it has a title page, issuing organization, report number, institutional publisher, or executive summary, you are probably looking at a report.

Do not use this page for:

- A peer-reviewed article in a journal PDF. Use [How to Cite a Journal Article](/guides/how-to-cite-a-journal-article/).
- A normal web article about a report. Use [How to Cite a Website](/guides/how-to-cite-a-website/).
- A book-length government publication that is cataloged as a book. Use [How to Cite a Book](/guides/how-to-cite-a-book/).
- A dissertation or thesis. That needs its own database, institution, and publication-status fields.

## Information to collect before you cite

Open the report itself, not just the page that links to it. Copy these fields:

- **Author** - a person, several people, a government agency, a company, or another organization.
- **Report title** - the full title and subtitle on the cover or title page.
- **Year or full date** - use the report publication date, not the date you downloaded it.
- **Report number** - NCES number, NIH publication number, working paper number, technical report number, or series number.
- **Publisher or issuing body** - the organization that published the report, if different from the author.
- **Parent agency** - useful for government reports with several agency layers.
- **URL or DOI** - use the DOI when the report has one; otherwise use the stable URL.
- **Access date** - required or expected by several styles for online reports.

[APA Style's government report example](https://apastyle.apa.org/style-grammar-guidelines/references/examples/report-government-agency-references) uses the specific agency as the author and places parent agencies in the source/publisher element. MLA's report guidance follows the same broad logic as the MLA core-elements template: author when there is one, title, publisher, date, and location.

The title is treated like a standalone work because the report can be found and read independently. The organization author matters because reports often have no personal byline. The report number matters because large agencies publish many documents with similar titles.

In-text examples:

- APA: (National Center for Education Statistics, 2023)
- MLA: (National Center for Education Statistics)
- Chicago author-date: (National Center for Education Statistics 2023)
- Harvard: (National Center for Education Statistics, 2023)
- Vancouver: (1)
- IEEE: [1]
- AMA: superscript reference number

## Government reports with agency layers

Government reports often have a specific office, a parent agency, and a department. Use the most specific body responsible for the report as the author, then use parent agencies as publisher or source information when the style supports it.

Good author:

> National Center for Education Statistics

Useful parent agencies:

> U.S. Department of Education, Institute of Education Sciences

Avoid turning the whole agency chain into a single author unless the report itself presents the full chain as the author. Long author names make in-text citations hard to read and usually duplicate publisher information.

If the report title page lists personal authors, use those people as authors and list the agency as the publisher. If it lists an editorial board or committee, use the committee if it is clearly responsible for the report.

## Annual reports, white papers, and technical reports

Not every report is a government document. The same rules apply, but the author/publisher decision changes.

**Annual report.** If a company writes and publishes its own annual report, the company is usually both author and publisher. Many styles avoid repeating the same organization twice.

> Example: Greenway Solar. *2025 Annual Report*. 2026. https://example.com/annual-report

**White paper.** A white paper may have individual authors, a company author, or a sponsoring organization. Use personal authors when named. Use the organization when no personal author is identified.

**Technical report.** Include the report number when present. IEEE, Vancouver, and AMA especially benefit from clear report-number placement because readers may search by that number.

**Working paper.** Treat the working paper series as source information when the style needs it. If a DOI exists, use the DOI.

**Policy brief.** Cite the brief like a short report. Keep the series or brief number if one is printed on the first page.

## Report number rules

A report number is not decoration. It can be the fastest way to find the exact source, especially when a government office publishes several versions of a similar report.

Common labels:

- Report No.
- Publication No.
- Technical Report
- Working Paper
- NCES number
- NIH Publication No.
- Series number

Place the number near the title. APA puts it in parentheses after the title. Vancouver and AMA can place it as a report number after the publisher/date information. IEEE commonly uses `Rep.` before the report number.

If no report number exists, do not invent one. Use the title, organization, year, and URL.

## Report or PDF?

Most report questions start because the source is a PDF. The answer is still report.

Use report format when the PDF has:

- A title page or cover
- An organization author
- A report number
- An issuing agency or publisher
- A formal publication date
- Sections such as executive summary, methods, findings, or recommendations

Use [PDF guidance](/guides/how-to-cite-a-pdf/) when you are deciding what kind of source the PDF actually is. Once you know it is a report, format it with the report rules on this page.

## Using the generator for reports

The [citation generator](/) can often extract the title, URL, site name, and date from a report landing page, but reports still need manual review. Report PDFs do not always expose clean metadata, and landing pages sometimes describe the report without including the exact report number.

After generating a citation:

1. Open the report PDF or title page.
2. Confirm the organization author.
3. Add the report number if the extractor missed it.
4. Replace a landing-page title with the report title if needed.
5. Add the parent agency or publisher when your style requires it.
6. Add an access date for styles that require one.

For DOI-bearing reports, use the DOI instead of a long URL. For reports with only a landing page and a PDF download, prefer the stable landing page when it contains the report metadata; use the PDF URL when the landing page is thin or temporary.

## Common mistakes

**Citing the PDF as a web page.** The URL is not the source type. A formal report deserves report fields.

**Using the parent agency as the author automatically.** Use the most specific responsible agency when the report identifies one.

**Dropping the report number.** If the report has an NCES, NIH, working paper, or technical report number, include it.

**Repeating the same organization twice.** If a company wrote and published its own annual report, many styles omit the repeated publisher.

**Using the download date as the publication date.** Your access date and the report's publication date are different.

**Ignoring a DOI.** Some reports and working papers have DOIs. Use the DOI when it exists.
