A DOI is a permanent identifier for a scholarly work. It usually looks like `10.1037/cogdev0000412` or `https://doi.org/10.1037/cogdev0000412`. If a journal article, preprint, data set, or report has a DOI, cite the DOI instead of a copy-pasted browser URL.

> The shortest answer: paste the DOI into the Journal tab of the generator, then verify the article title, journal title, year, volume, issue, page range, and DOI against the article landing page or PDF.

## What a DOI does

DOI stands for digital object identifier. It is not just a link; it is a registered identifier that should keep pointing to the source even when the publisher changes its website. The [DOI Foundation](https://www.doi.org/the-identifier/what-is-a-doi/) describes the DOI as a persistent identifier for a digital object; citation styles use that persistence to avoid brittle publisher URLs.

Use the DOI for:

- Peer-reviewed journal articles
- Advance online journal articles
- Preprints with registered DOIs
- Data sets
- Reports or standards with registered DOIs
- Book chapters that have their own DOI

Do not use the DOI field for a normal web page that only has a URL. If the source has no DOI, cite the stable URL instead.

## Where to find a DOI

Look for the DOI on the article landing page before you open the PDF. Publishers usually place it near the title, abstract, issue details, or "cite this article" button. In a PDF, the DOI often appears on the first page near the copyright line or article history.

Good DOI locations:

- The journal article landing page
- The first page of the PDF
- A "cite" or "export citation" panel on the publisher site
- Crossref or DataCite metadata
- PubMed records that link out to the publisher

Do not copy a library proxy link, database permalink, or session URL when a DOI is available. A DOI may route you through a paywall, but it still identifies the work more cleanly than a temporary database URL.

If you only have a DOI string, normalize it before citing. `doi:10.1037/cogdev0000412`, `https://dx.doi.org/10.1037/cogdev0000412`, and `https://doi.org/10.1037/cogdev0000412` all point to the same DOI. Most modern styles that use DOI URLs prefer the `https://doi.org/` form.

## DOI format by citation style

The same DOI can appear in different visible forms depending on the style.

| Style | DOI format |
| --- | --- |
| [APA 7](/guides/apa/) | `https://doi.org/10.1037/cogdev0000412` |
| [MLA 9](/guides/mla/) | `https://doi.org/10.1037/cogdev0000412` |
| [Chicago 18](/guides/chicago/) | `https://doi.org/10.1037/cogdev0000412` |
| [Harvard](/guides/harvard/) | `Available at: https://doi.org/10.1037/cogdev0000412` |
| [Vancouver](/guides/vancouver/) | `doi:10.1037/cogdev0000412` |
| [IEEE](/guides/ieee/) | `doi: 10.1037/cogdev0000412` |
| [AMA 11](/guides/ama/) | `doi:10.1037/cogdev0000412` |

The DOI itself is the part beginning with `10.`. The prefix around it is style-specific.

## How to use the generator with a DOI

1. Copy the DOI from the article page or PDF. Either `10.1037/cogdev0000412` or `https://doi.org/10.1037/cogdev0000412` is fine.
2. Open the Journal tab on the [citation generator](/).
3. Paste the DOI and choose your citation style.
4. Review the generated citation.
5. Edit any missing volume, issue, page, or date fields before submitting your work.

The DOI lookup uses scholarly metadata providers, but publisher metadata is not perfect. The most common missing fields are issue number, page range, and advance-online publication status.

## DOI versus URL

Use the DOI when both a DOI and a normal URL are available.

Good:

> https://doi.org/10.1037/cogdev0000412

Usually not the best citation URL:

> https://www.publisher.example.com/article/12345?utm_source=newsletter

The publisher URL may still be useful for reading the article, but the DOI is the cleaner citation link. It also avoids tracking parameters, session IDs, and access-specific paths.

There are two exceptions to watch for. First, do not invent a DOI from a publisher URL; use only a DOI that the source or a registry actually provides. Second, if you are citing a web page about an article rather than the article itself - for example, a news release summarizing a study - cite the web page URL, not the study DOI, unless your sentence is actually using the study.

## Missing volume, issue, or pages

Some DOI records describe an article before the final issue assignment is available. If the article is marked "advance online publication," "online first," or "early view," cite the DOI and omit volume, issue, and page range until the final issue version appears.

If the landing page or PDF already shows volume, issue, and pages but the generator does not, add those fields manually. The citation should match the published article, not the incomplete DOI registry record.

## DOI metadata can still be wrong

A DOI lookup is a strong starting point, not a final proofread. DOI registries receive metadata from publishers, and that metadata can be incomplete, stale, or formatted for machine exchange rather than student papers.

Check these fields before you submit:

- **Article title** - capitalization should match your citation style, not necessarily the publisher's all-caps display.
- **Journal title** - use the full title for APA, MLA, Chicago, and Harvard; use the correct abbreviation for Vancouver, IEEE, and AMA when your style calls for it.
- **Publication date** - distinguish advance online publication from final issue publication.
- **Volume, issue, and pages** - add them when the final issue version exists.
- **Author order** - do not alphabetize authors; keep the order from the article.

The generator can speed up the boring part. The final citation is still your responsibility, especially when a professor, journal, or department has a local rule that differs from the generic style.

## Common DOI mistakes

**Using both DOI and URL.** Most styles do not want both for a DOI-bearing journal article. Use the DOI.

**Leaving tracking parameters in the DOI URL.** `https://doi.org/10.1037/cogdev0000412?utm_source=x` should be cited as `https://doi.org/10.1037/cogdev0000412`.

**Adding a period inside the DOI.** Sentence punctuation belongs after the citation, not inside the DOI itself.

**Using the article database URL.** A library proxy URL is not the source identifier. Use the DOI from the article page.

**Assuming every long number is a DOI.** A PMID, ISBN, ISSN, arXiv ID, and DOI are different identifiers. A DOI begins with `10.` followed by a registrant code and a slash.
