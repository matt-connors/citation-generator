Web pages often hide authorship. A news story may have a visible byline, but a help article, public-health explainer, university page, product documentation page, or government fact sheet may not name a person at all. That does not mean the citation is optional; it means you have to decide whether the page has an organization author or no author.

> The shortest answer: if a web page has no personal or organization author, start the citation with the page title. Do not invent an author, and do not use the website name as the author unless that organization is clearly responsible for the content.

## Check for an organization author first

Before you call a website author-less, look for a responsible organization. Many web pages do not name a person because the author is institutional.

Good places to check:

- The page header or footer
- The "About" page
- The copyright line
- A "reviewed by" or "content owner" note
- The page URL path, especially on government and university sites
- JSON-LD or metadata shown by a citation generator

If the page is on `cdc.gov` and the content is a CDC fact sheet, the author is the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention. If it is a university admissions page, the university or admissions office is usually the author. If it is a corporate support article, the company is usually the author. Use no-author formatting only when no person or organization takes responsibility for the content.

[APA Style's webpage reference examples](https://apastyle.apa.org/style-grammar-guidelines/references/examples/webpage-website-references) make this distinction directly: a page from an organization's website can use the organization as the author, and when the organization and site name are the same, the repeated site name is omitted. [MLA Style Center's no-author guidance](https://style.mla.org/source-with-no-author/) takes the other branch: when no author exists, begin the Works Cited entry with the title, not with "Anonymous."

That first decision matters more than punctuation. A page with an organization author and a page with no author produce different in-text citations, different alphabetization, and sometimes a different source element.

## Basic no-author format

When there is truly no author, the title moves into the author position. The rest of the citation stays in the normal website order for your style.

Example source:

> Page title: "Student Sleep Patterns During Final Exams"  
> Site name: Campus Wellness Review  
> Date: April 9, 2024  
> URL: https://example.edu/wellness/student-sleep-patterns

| Style | Reference list entry |
| --- | --- |
| [APA 7](/guides/apa/) | Student sleep patterns during final exams. (2024, April 9). *Campus Wellness Review*. https://example.edu/wellness/student-sleep-patterns |
| [MLA 9](/guides/mla/) | "Student Sleep Patterns During Final Exams." *Campus Wellness Review*, 9 Apr. 2024, https://example.edu/wellness/student-sleep-patterns. |
| [Chicago 18](/guides/chicago/) | "Student Sleep Patterns During Final Exams." 2024. *Campus Wellness Review*, April 9, 2024. https://example.edu/wellness/student-sleep-patterns. |
| [Harvard](/guides/harvard/) | *Student sleep patterns during final exams* (2024) *Campus Wellness Review*. Available at: https://example.edu/wellness/student-sleep-patterns (Accessed: 26 June 2026). |
| [Vancouver](/guides/vancouver/) | Student sleep patterns during final exams. Campus Wellness Review [Internet]. 2024 Apr 9 [cited 2026 Jun 26]. Available from: https://example.edu/wellness/student-sleep-patterns |
| [IEEE](/guides/ieee/) | "Student Sleep Patterns During Final Exams," *Campus Wellness Review*, Apr. 9, 2024. Accessed: Jun. 26, 2026. [Online]. Available: https://example.edu/wellness/student-sleep-patterns |
| [AMA 11](/guides/ama/) | Student sleep patterns during final exams. *Campus Wellness Review*. Published April 9, 2024. Accessed June 26, 2026. https://example.edu/wellness/student-sleep-patterns |

The title is not a substitute author; it is just the first retrievable element. Alphabetize the entry by the first significant word of the title, ignoring *A*, *An*, and *The* when your style uses alphabetical reference lists.

## Style-by-style decision rules

Use this checklist before you format the final entry.

| Style | If there is no personal author | In-text citation shape |
| --- | --- | --- |
| APA 7 | Use an organization as author if it owns the page; otherwise move the title to the author position. | Organization/year or shortened title/year |
| MLA 9 | Start with the title when no author is named; use a corporate author when the organization created the work. | First element of the Works Cited entry |
| Chicago 18 | Start with the title when no author is available; keep organization authors when clear. | Title/year in author-date; note citation in notes-bibliography |
| Harvard | Use the organization if responsible; otherwise start with the title. | Organization/year or title/year |
| Vancouver | Number the source in citation order; the reference entry starts with the title when no author is present. | Numeric citation |
| IEEE | Number the source in citation order; the reference entry can begin with the title. | Numeric bracket |
| AMA 11 | Number the source in citation order; use the title first when no author is present. | Superscript reference number |

Numeric styles hide the no-author problem in the body because the in-text citation is just a number. The reference entry still needs a stable first element, and that first element is usually the title.

## In-text citations with no author

In-text citations use a shortened title. Keep enough words that the reader can find the matching reference list entry quickly.

| Style | In-text example |
| --- | --- |
| APA 7 | ("Student Sleep Patterns," 2024) |
| MLA 9 | ("Student Sleep Patterns") |
| Chicago author-date | ("Student Sleep Patterns" 2024) |
| Harvard | ("Student sleep patterns during final exams", 2024) |
| Vancouver | (1) |
| IEEE | [1] |
| AMA | Superscript reference number |

APA and Chicago put article or page titles in quotation marks in the in-text citation. MLA does the same for short works such as articles and web pages. If the no-author source is a whole website or a long standalone report, italicize the title in styles that italicize whole works.

## No author, but an organization is responsible

Do not move the title forward when an organization is clearly the author.

Example:

> Centers for Disease Control and Prevention. "About Adult BMI." CDC, 2024, https://www.cdc.gov/bmi/adult-calculator/index.html.

The CDC is the author because the page is official agency content. The website name may also be CDC, but that does not remove the author. If the organization name and site name are identical, some styles omit the repeated site or publisher name to avoid redundancy; the dedicated [APA](/guides/apa/), [MLA](/guides/mla/), and [Chicago](/guides/chicago/) guides cover those style-specific details.

Institutional authors are especially common on:

- Government fact sheets
- University policy pages
- Hospital and public-health explainers
- Product documentation
- Standards pages
- Nonprofit reports

Do not overread a generic footer. "Copyright Example University" is not enough by itself to make Example University the author of every page on the site. Look for page-level responsibility: a named department, content owner, reviewed-by note, report cover, or official page context.

## What to put in the generator

If you use the [citation generator](/), choose the website source type and check the author field after extraction.

- If a person is named, keep the person as the author.
- If an organization clearly owns the page, enter the organization in the author field.
- If no person or organization is responsible, leave the author field blank and make sure the title is correct.
- If the generator imported the site name as the author, confirm that the site is actually the responsible organization before keeping it.

This is one of the few web-citation cases where manual review matters more than automatic extraction. Metadata often stores `site_name`, `publisher`, and `author` in overlapping fields, and websites do not use those labels consistently.

## No author and no date

Combine the two rules. Start with the title, then apply the no-date convention for your style.

Example source:

> Page title: "Campus Dining Allergen Policy"  
> Site name: Student Services Handbook  
> No visible publication date  
> URL: https://example.edu/student-services/dining/allergens

APA:

> Campus dining allergen policy. (n.d.). *Student Services Handbook*. https://example.edu/student-services/dining/allergens

MLA:

> "Campus Dining Allergen Policy." *Student Services Handbook*, https://example.edu/student-services/dining/allergens. Accessed 26 June 2026.

Chicago author-date:

> "Campus Dining Allergen Policy." n.d. *Student Services Handbook*. Accessed June 26, 2026. https://example.edu/student-services/dining/allergens.

Date-specific examples belong in the dedicated no-date guide, which is scheduled after this no-author guide in the publishing queue.

## Alphabetizing no-author entries

When a reference list is alphabetical, sort the entry by the first significant word in the title. Ignore leading articles such as *A*, *An*, and *The* if your style's alphabetizing rules tell you to do so.

Example:

> "The Campus Dining Allergen Policy" sorts under **Campus**, not **The**.

For MLA, the in-text citation also follows the first element of the Works Cited entry. If the full entry starts with "Campus Dining Allergen Policy," the parenthetical citation should use a shortened title that clearly points to that entry:

> ("Campus Dining")

Do not shorten the title so aggressively that two entries become indistinguishable. If your Works Cited page has "Campus Dining Allergen Policy" and "Campus Dining Hours," use enough words to separate them.

## Common mistakes

**Using the site name as the author automatically.** The site name is not always the author. A page on a newspaper site may be written by a reporter; a page on a university site may be written by a department; a page on a wiki may be collaboratively edited and effectively author-less.

**Writing "Anonymous."** Use Anonymous only when the page explicitly says the author is Anonymous. Otherwise, begin with the title.

**Leaving the title out.** The title becomes more important when no author exists. It is how the reader matches the in-text citation to the reference list.

**Alphabetizing under the website name.** If the entry starts with the title, alphabetize under the title.

**Trusting generated metadata without checking the page.** Automatic citation extraction can miss hidden authors or mistake the site name for an author. After using the [citation generator](/), open the edit form and confirm that the author field is either a real person, a responsible organization, or intentionally blank.

## Quick checklist

1. Look for a person byline.
2. Look for an organization that owns or reviews the page.
3. If neither exists, move the page title to the first position.
4. Use a shortened title for the in-text citation.
5. Apply the normal website format for the remaining fields.
6. If the page also has no date, use your style's no-date rule.
