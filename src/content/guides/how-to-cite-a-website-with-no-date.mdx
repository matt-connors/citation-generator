A web page with no date is not incomplete research, but it does need a careful citation. Dates tell readers which version of an online source you used. When the page does not show a publication date, each style has a fallback: some write `n.d.`, some omit the date, and most add an access date because the page can change later.

> The shortest answer: if a web page has no publication or page-specific update date, use `n.d.` in APA, Chicago, and Harvard, omit the publication date in MLA when needed, and include an access date in styles that require or expect one for web pages.

## Make sure the page is really undated

Before you use a no-date citation, check whether the date is simply somewhere else on the page.

Look for:

- A byline date near the title
- A "last updated," "reviewed," or "revised" note
- A report cover date if the page links to a PDF
- A schema or metadata date read by a citation generator
- A permanent archive snapshot date when citing an archived version

Do not use a site-wide copyright year as the page date unless it clearly belongs to the page itself. A footer that says `Copyright 2026 Example University` usually means the website is current, not that the page was published in 2026.

APA's official [webpage reference examples](https://apastyle.apa.org/style-grammar-guidelines/references/examples/webpage-website-references) emphasize page-specific dates and warn against treating review dates as publication dates when the content has not necessarily changed. That rule is useful outside APA too: cite the date attached to the work, not a decorative site date.

## Dates that count and dates that do not

Use a date only when it identifies the page or source you are citing.

| Date label | Usually cite it? | Why |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Published | Yes | It identifies when the page or article first appeared. |
| Last updated | Usually | Use it when the update belongs to the page content. |
| Revised | Usually | It normally signals a content change. |
| Last reviewed | Not by itself | Review can mean someone checked the page without changing it. |
| Copyright | Rarely | It often applies to the whole site, not the specific page. |
| Accessed | Yes, when required | It records when you viewed an online source. |
| Search-result date | No | It may be crawler metadata, not a page date. |

When two page-specific dates appear, choose the one your style or assignment asks for. For normal web pages, a specific publication date is usually better than a generic update note. For reference pages that are maintained over time, the updated or revised date may be the best available date.

## Basic no-date format

Example source:

> Page title: "Campus Dining Allergen Policy"  
> Site name: Student Services Handbook  
> No visible publication date  
> URL: https://example.edu/student-services/dining/allergens  
> Accessed: June 26, 2026

| Style | Reference list entry |
| --- | --- |
| [APA 7](/guides/apa/) | Campus dining allergen policy. (n.d.). *Student Services Handbook*. https://example.edu/student-services/dining/allergens |
| [MLA 9](/guides/mla/) | "Campus Dining Allergen Policy." *Student Services Handbook*, https://example.edu/student-services/dining/allergens. Accessed 26 June 2026. |
| [Chicago 18](/guides/chicago/) | "Campus Dining Allergen Policy." n.d. *Student Services Handbook*. Accessed June 26, 2026. https://example.edu/student-services/dining/allergens. |
| [Harvard](/guides/harvard/) | *Campus dining allergen policy* (no date) *Student Services Handbook*. Available at: https://example.edu/student-services/dining/allergens (Accessed: 26 June 2026). |
| [Vancouver](/guides/vancouver/) | Campus dining allergen policy. Student Services Handbook [Internet]. [cited 2026 Jun 26]. Available from: https://example.edu/student-services/dining/allergens |
| [IEEE](/guides/ieee/) | "Campus Dining Allergen Policy," *Student Services Handbook*. Accessed: Jun. 26, 2026. [Online]. Available: https://example.edu/student-services/dining/allergens |
| [AMA 11](/guides/ama/) | Campus dining allergen policy. *Student Services Handbook*. Accessed June 26, 2026. https://example.edu/student-services/dining/allergens |

The phrase changes by style. APA uses `(n.d.)`; Chicago author-date commonly uses `n.d.` in the year position; Harvard often writes `(no date)`; MLA omits the publication date and relies on the access date. Numeric styles usually emphasize the cited/accessed date rather than forcing `n.d.` into the entry.

## No-date website examples with an author

No-date rules do not always mean no-author rules. If the page names an author, keep the author and replace only the missing date.

Example source:

> Author: Jordan Lee  
> Page title: "Planning a Community Oral History Project"  
> Site name: Local Archives Toolkit  
> No visible publication date  
> URL: https://example.org/oral-history/community-project

| Style | Reference list entry |
| --- | --- |
| APA 7 | Lee, J. (n.d.). *Planning a community oral history project*. Local Archives Toolkit. https://example.org/oral-history/community-project |
| MLA 9 | Lee, Jordan. "Planning a Community Oral History Project." *Local Archives Toolkit*, https://example.org/oral-history/community-project. Accessed 26 June 2026. |
| Chicago 18 | Lee, Jordan. n.d. "Planning a Community Oral History Project." *Local Archives Toolkit*. Accessed June 26, 2026. https://example.org/oral-history/community-project. |
| Harvard | Lee, J. (no date) "Planning a community oral history project", *Local Archives Toolkit*. Available at: https://example.org/oral-history/community-project (Accessed: 26 June 2026). |

The in-text citation still starts with the author:

> APA: (Lee, n.d.)  
> MLA: (Lee)  
> Harvard: (Lee, no date)

If there is no author either, use the title-first pattern in [How to Cite a Website With No Author](/guides/how-to-cite-a-website-with-no-author/).

## In-text citations with no date

When your style uses author-date in-text citations, the no-date marker appears where the year would normally go.

| Style | In-text example |
| --- | --- |
| APA 7 | ("Campus Dining Allergen Policy," n.d.) |
| Chicago author-date | ("Campus Dining Allergen Policy" n.d.) |
| Harvard | (*Campus dining allergen policy*, no date) |
| MLA 9 | ("Campus Dining Allergen Policy") |
| Vancouver | (1) |
| IEEE | [1] |
| AMA | Superscript reference number |

If the page has an author, keep the author in the in-text citation and replace only the date:

> APA: (Centers for Disease Control and Prevention, n.d.)

If the page has no author either, shorten the title. See [How to Cite a Website With No Author](/guides/how-to-cite-a-website-with-no-author/) for that combined case.

## Published date vs. updated date

Use the published date when the page shows both "published" and "updated." The published date identifies when the work first appeared. An updated date can matter if the update changed the facts you are relying on, but it is not always the date styles expect in the main publication-date slot.

Use the updated date when:

- There is no publication date.
- The page labels the date as "last reviewed," "last updated," or "revised."
- The update appears to describe the specific page content, not the whole site.

Do not use:

- A footer copyright year.
- A dynamic current-year copyright notice.
- A date from a sidebar that belongs to a different article.
- A date from search results when the page itself does not show one.

## Access dates

An access date records when you saw the page. It matters most when the page has no publication date, changes often, or lacks a stable archive.

MLA, Harvard, Vancouver, IEEE, and AMA normally include access dates for web references. APA and Chicago are more selective: include one for pages designed to change, pages with no visible date, wiki entries, live dashboards, and any page where the version matters.

Use a full date, not just a month or year:

> Accessed 26 June 2026.  
> Accessed June 26, 2026.  
> [cited 2026 Jun 26].

If your instructor gives a house style, follow it. Access-date rules are one of the places where departments often add local requirements.

## How to use the generator with no-date pages

Automatic extraction may pull a date from metadata even when the visible page does not show one. That date might be useful, but check it before trusting it.

When the [citation generator](/) returns a date for a page that appears undated:

1. Look for the same date on the page itself.
2. Check whether the date belongs to the article, a sidebar, or the whole site.
3. Remove the date if it is only a copyright year or crawler timestamp.
4. Add an access date if your style requires one.
5. Use `n.d.`, `no date`, or date omission according to your style.

The generated citation should reflect the source a reader can verify. Hidden metadata is helpful only when it matches the page-level evidence.

## No date, but the source is still credible

A missing date does not automatically make a source unusable. Some stable reference pages do not change often and do not show publication dates. Others are weak sources because the lack of date makes the information impossible to evaluate.

Use an undated web page cautiously when:

- The topic changes quickly, such as health guidance, law, technology, or statistics.
- The page makes factual claims without references.
- The page gives advice but does not identify a reviewer or organization.
- The page has no author, no date, and no editorial owner.

For fast-changing topics, prefer a dated report, journal article, government page, or archived version. A citation can format an undated page correctly; it cannot make the source stronger than it is.

## When to find a better source

Use a different source when the date is central to your claim. A no-date page might be acceptable for a stable definition, a university policy, or a general background explainer. It is weaker for claims about:

- Current medical guidance
- Legal rules or court procedures
- Tuition, financial aid, or admissions requirements
- Technology versions and software instructions
- Statistics, rankings, and market data
- News, elections, and public policy

If you must use an undated source for a changing topic, mention the access date in your notes and save a PDF or archive snapshot for your records. That will not fix a weak source, but it gives you evidence of the version you saw.

## Common mistakes

**Using today's date as the publication date.** Today's date is your access date, not the publication date.

**Using the copyright year as the page date.** A site-wide copyright line does not prove when the page was written or updated.

**Dropping the access date when the style expects one.** For no-date web pages, the access date is often the only time marker in the citation.

**Writing "unknown date."** Use the style's convention: `n.d.`, `no date`, or date omission.

**Forgetting to update the in-text citation.** In author-date styles, the in-text citation also needs the no-date marker.

## Quick checklist

1. Search the page for published, updated, reviewed, or revised dates.
2. Ignore site-wide copyright years unless they clearly describe the page.
3. Use the page-specific updated date when there is no publication date.
4. If no date exists, apply your style's no-date rule.
5. Include an access date when your style requires one or the page may change.
6. Verify the generated citation before submitting.
